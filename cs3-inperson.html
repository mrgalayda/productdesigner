01
Origin

Where It
Started

In 2020, someone spotted something the rest of the org hadn't: Braintree's ecommerce platform had no answer for the physical world.

In-person payments account for 80% of all global transactions. And virtually all of them were processed by competing PSPs. A merchant who wanted a true omnichannel experience had to stitch together Braintree for ecommerce and a separate provider like Verifone for in-person. Fragmented, expensive, and an obvious gap.

A demo was built with an engineer, got it funded, and what existed was a sketch: a rudimentary payment app running on Verifone hardware. That's when I was brought in.

I didn't start this project. But I'm the reason it shipped.

03
Research

Secret Shopping
During COVID

Most designers do user research in a lab or over Zoom. I did mine at cash registers.

During COVID, when in-person retail was stripped back to its essentials, I spent days as a secret shopper observing real payment moments in real environments.

Omnichannel Return

Customers frustrated trying to return an online purchase in-store, only to find the systems weren't connected. The omnichannel promise, broken in public.

Sephora

The complexity of coupons, promotional rates, and rewards points converging at a single swipe, graceful when it worked, chaotic when it didn't.

Amazon Go

Minimal friction. Invisible payment. A checkout that felt like the future, setting the bar for everything I was about to design.

The best in-person payment experiences are the ones you barely notice. The worst make you feel like the system wasn't built for you. I designed toward the former.

04
Vision

Beyond the
Transaction

The brief was to build a functional payment app. I saw something bigger in the real estate we were about to occupy.

Every card reader is a moment of contact between a merchant and their customer. That moment is almost always wasted: a generic prompt, a beep, a receipt. I wanted to change that.

01
Personalization
Personalized Checkout

A swipe or rewards card surfaces a personalized experience mid-transaction. Forgot your wallet? Show your phone and the system connects to your vaulted Visa. The cashier closes the sale. You never felt the friction.

02
Loyalty
Rewards Integration

Silently connects to loyalty accounts in the background. No second scan, no extra app. Rewards accrue automatically. Merchants retain the relationship. Customers feel taken care of.

03
Margin
Branded Payments

Once Braintree owned the checkout real estate, the terminal became a natural surface for PayPal's high-margin branded methods, including PayPal, Venmo, and BNPL, exactly where purchase intent is highest.

04
Community
Charitable Giving

PayPal Giving Fund as round-up donations at checkout. One tap at the end of a transaction. No new app, no separate flow. A native part of the experience.

These weren't isolated features. They were a vision for what a payment terminal becomes when design is involved from the start: a relationship tool, not just a transaction tool.

05
Hardware

Designing for
the Device

The Verifone terminal came with all of the complexities of a payment acceptance device that sought to accept payments wherever and however the customer so chooses.

This wasn't a screen design problem. It was a hardware design problem. The device had to work in a restaurant, a retail store, a pop-up, a stadium concession stand. It had to handle tap, swipe, chip, and PIN. It had to work when the network was slow, when the merchant was distracted, and when the customer had never seen a Braintree-powered terminal before.

Every payment method carried its own flow. Every flow had to feel like one product. Chip and PIN required different timing than contactless. Swipe required fallback logic. NFC required trust signals that tap-and-go users hadn't been trained to expect. The device had to be forgiving, fast, and clear — in every configuration, for every merchant type, in every physical environment.

The Challenge
One Device, Every Context

Tap, swipe, chip, PIN, NFC, and fallback flows — each with distinct timing, trust signals, and error states. All had to feel like a single, coherent product regardless of how the customer chose to pay.

The Constraint
Hardware Limits Design

Unlike a web product, you can't push a fix. Screen size, processing speed, and input method were fixed. Every design decision had to account for the physical and technical constraints of the terminal itself.

Designing for physical hardware also meant designing for the environment around it. Counter height. Lighting. The customer's line of sight. Whether the merchant was watching or had walked away. These aren't considerations that come up in a typical product design brief — but they determined whether the experience felt right or wrong in the real world.

06
Candor

Constraints &
What I'd Do
Differently

This was one of the most rewarding projects of my career and a clear example of what sustained execution looks like without a team. The project began solo and grew to three designers as the product proved its value.

I asked for a team. I didn't get one. The work spanned UX, documentation, developer relations, product strategy, and field research, genuinely multi-person work that I did solo because there was no other option.

The more ambitious ideas were fully designed and articulated but not completely built. Not because they were wrong, but because there wasn't the staff to execute them. New leadership, shifting priorities, and the organizational reality that projects without a quarterly win lose momentum also played a role.

We delivered an enormous amount. But the vision was larger than what one designer and a small team could fully realize in the time available.

We shipped something real from nearly nothing. I know exactly how much further the right investment would have taken it.
